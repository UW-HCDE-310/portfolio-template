Teaching English as a Foreign Language (EFL) in Japan

I interviewed five native Japanese speakers to understand the difficulties they faced in the classroom, using apps, and with the English language. My research findings will be uploaded soon, as will the prototypes that Gabi Cronk designed based on my research findings.

Background information

Since the introduction of computer assisted language learning (CALL) in the 1990s, people have been using technology to help with their study of foreign languages. Various studies have confirmed both CALL and mobile assisted language learning (MALL) do help students improve language skills.

However, many MALL and CALL systems focus on vocabulary memorization through brute-force repetition. While memorization is an important tool and it is important for students to expand their vocabulary, it is more important that students are able to create new sentences themself. Translation based curriculum (e.g., dragging words into the correct order or typing a full sentence on Duolingo) has the same drawback.
